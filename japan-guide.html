Multi-share is the trade

4 nights are hostel multi-share (4–6 to a room, shared bathrooms) — the known compromise for these dates + price + crowd. Japan's hostels are famously clean and orderly; bring earplugs & an eye mask and it's very manageable.

Staying on protocol

The Shred, in Japan

Japan might be the single best food culture for a cut — lean protein everywhere, controlled portions, low-oil cooking. Here's how to stay locked in while still eating the good stuff.

🐟

Sashimi & grilled fish

Sashimi, grilled salmon/mackerel, yakitori — high protein, low carb, minimal oil. The default win at any conveyor-sushi or izakaya.

🍢

Yakitori & izakaya

Skewered grilled chicken (go for momo, sasami, negima) is lean protein by the stick. Order grilled over fried, skip the rice-heavy sides.

🥚

Konbini is your friend

7-Eleven/Lawson have boiled eggs, salad chicken (sarada chikin), edamame, Greek yogurt — perfect macro-friendly grabs between sights. A Shred traveller's secret weapon.

🍜

The carb traps

Ramen, gyudon, katsu, mochi — delicious, carb/oil heavy. Enjoy them intentionally on the big nights (First Night Out, Kyoto), pre-logged, not by default.

🚶

Steps handle cardio

Tokyo & Kyoto are huge walking cities — you'll easily clock 15–20k steps/day. That's your NEAT cardio sorted without a gym. Knee-friendly: it's flat, paved walking.

🍺

The night-out plan

Two built-in nights out (Tokyo, Kyoto karaoke). Highballs & shochu-soda are lower-cal than beer/sake. Hydrate, enjoy them as your treat windows, tighten around them.

What you'll eat

The table

From conveyor sushi to Osaka's street food — and plenty for vegetarians too (handy for the veg eaters back home). The shortlist worth chasing.

Sushi & Sashimi

Conveyor-belt (kaiten) sushi in Tokyo is cheap, fresh, and endlessly fun. Tsukiji Outer Market for the real deal.

Ramen

Each region its own style — Tokyo shoyu, rich tonkotsu. A once-or-twice treat on the trip. Ticket-machine shops are the move.

Okonomiyaki & Takoyaki

Osaka's soul food — savoury pancakes and octopus balls in Dotonbori. Your solo-tail must-eat.

Hida Beef

Takayama's famous wagyu — try it grilled or as sushi on Sake Street. A regional splurge on your ryokan day.

Vegetarian

Shojin Ryori

Buddhist temple cuisine in Kyoto — elegant, entirely plant-based, deeply satisfying. A Kyoto highlight for veg eaters.

Vegetarian

Veg ramen & tofu

Vegetable/miso ramen, agedashi tofu, vegetable tempura, inari sushi — Japan has rich meat-free options if you ask (look for "yasai").

🍵

A Kyoto ritual worth booking: a traditional tea ceremony (matcha + wagashi) — one of the tour's suggested free-time activities, and a genuinely lovely, low-key contrast to the neon and karaoke. Calm, cultural, and very Kyoto.

Pack & prep

The carry-on rules

The Shinkansen luggage cap means pack light — a soft carry-on (under 160cm total) plus a day pack. You carry your own bag through stations and up stairs, so travel lean.

👕 Summer kit

  • Light breathable tees, shorts/light trousers
  • One smart-casual set for nights out
  • Compact umbrella + light rain layer
  • Modest layer for temples (covers shoulders/knees)

👟 Feet

  • Broken-in walking trainers (15–20k steps/day)
  • Easy slip-on shoes (temples & ryokan require removal)
  • Sandals/flip-flops

🎒 The bag

  • Soft carry-on, under 160cm total (Shinkansen rule)
  • Day pack for daily essentials
  • Packing cubes — small rooms, multi-share
  • Earplugs + eye mask (hostel nights)

🔌 Tech & docs

  • Type A adapter (100V), power bank
  • eSIM or pocket wifi; set up Mobile Suica
  • Cash + a backup card (7-Eleven ATMs)
  • Printed e-visa, insurance, G voucher
